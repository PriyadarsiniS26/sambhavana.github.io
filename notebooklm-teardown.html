NotebookLM Isn't a Better ChatGPT.
That's the Point.

A PM analysis of Google's most underestimated AI product, its philosophy, competitive position, trust architecture, and what I'd actually measure.

I killed three pothos before I did what I should have done from the start: actually researched the problem properly. I uploaded a Reddit thread to NotebookLM. What happened next is what this teardown is really about.

It didn't generate a generic answer. It showed me exactly which comments disagreed with each other, which diagnosis had the most thread support, and flagged the one outlier argument about tap water minerals that everyone else had ignored. It organized chaos I had already collected rather than generating new chaos I'd have to verify.

That distinction, between organizing what you know and generating what you don't, is NotebookLM's entire product philosophy. And it's worth understanding precisely, because most people are either underusing it or using it for the wrong job entirely.

Where It Sits in the Market

The honest competitive frame for NotebookLM isn't ChatGPT. It's Perplexity.

Both do research. Both cite sources. The difference is architectural: Perplexity operates as an open system with access to the entire internet, while NotebookLM operates as a closed system that only knows about the documents you've uploaded, and that limitation is actually its greatest strength for certain use cases.

Perplexity is designed to help cut down on the time you spend searching for information. NotebookLM's work comes in after you've gathered your information. They're not substitutes. They're sequential.

The most sophisticated users are running Perplexity to discover sources and NotebookLM to interrogate them. The fact that Google had to build Deep Research to patch the "I don't have sources yet" gap is evidence that the product's original positioning assumed a user who already knew how to find material. Most users don't.

What It Does Genuinely Well

Format fluency

Serious research rarely lives in one format. A journalist's source base might be recorded interviews, court PDFs, and web articles simultaneously. NotebookLM ingests all of it, PDFs, websites, YouTube videos, audio files, Google Docs, Slides, Sheets, .docx, treats every format equally, and synthesizes across them. That's a real capability gap versus tools that are text-only or require format conversion.

Audio and video as research input, not just output

This is the product's most undermarketed capability. Upload a recorded interview, a lecture, a mock presentation, and you can interrogate it the way you'd interrogate a document. What argument did the speaker make? Where did the logic weaken? What went unanswered?

I tested this directly. I uploaded a YouTube documentary on Unit 731. When I pushed into adjacent territory, the Rape of Nanking, post-war immunity decisions, patterns of wartime sexual violence, the product flagged where its source ran thin, offered to retrieve additional material, and continued the analysis when asked. All of it cited, all of it traceable.

The product markets Audio Overview, turning documents into audio, far more than it markets the inverse: turning audio into an analyzable research source. That's a positioning gap worth closing.

Output flexibility

Sources come in across formats and go out as study guides, briefings, mind maps, flashcards, audio overviews, video overviews, and custom reports. Audio overviews alone offer four formats, Deep Dive, Brief, Critique, and Debate, each serving a different analytical purpose. A teacher creates a podcast from course materials. A consultant turns a client document set into a briefing. A researcher turns 40 papers into a mind map. Same product, different outputs, same source-accountability model throughout.

Real institutional traction

In 2025, Google integrated NotebookLM into over 1,000 US higher education institutions. NYU's Wagner School uses it in a graduate public policy course, students build literature reviews from a curated source base and check written drafts against those sources for accuracy across a full semester. That embedded, longitudinal use is exactly the behavior the product needs to drive retention.

Why Google Built This

NotebookLM is not Google's revenue product. Understanding why Google built it anyway matters for reading every product decision it makes.

NotebookLM is three things simultaneously: a Gemini capability showcase, an enterprise adoption vehicle, and a strategic counter to the narrative that Google is losing the AI research race. Every institutional integration pulls educators and students deeper into Google's ecosystem. Every notebook built on Gemini demonstrates that Google's model can handle complex, multi-source, long-context reasoning.

The strategic logic

The product doesn't need to generate revenue directly. It needs to win the workflows of the users most likely to influence enterprise AI adoption decisions: researchers, educators, journalists, policy professionals. That clarity explains why Google has invested in institutional integrations rather than consumer growth hacks.

The Trust Architecture Problem

NotebookLM operates in two modes with meaningfully different trust properties, and it doesn't signal the transition between them.

Mode 1: You supply the sources. You selected them, you can defend them, the accountability is yours. The source-accountability promise holds completely.

Mode 2: Deep Research. You give NotebookLM a question. It builds a research plan, browses hundreds of websites, and returns a structured report alongside a full list of cited and uncited sources. The output transparency is genuine, every claim is traceable, and the product shows you what it used and what it found but didn't use.

What isn't disclosed is the selection logic that produced that source list. Google describes Deep Research as finding "high-quality sources" without defining what that means technically. Since Deep Research is effectively a search engine, results are easily swayed by SEO optimization and popular sentiment. Well-optimized content has a structural advantage over authoritative content that isn't, which describes a significant portion of academic literature, primary sources, and specialized professional material.

The tool hands users the list and the responsibility simultaneously, without disclosing how the list was generated or what might be systematically missing from it. For a product whose institutional strategy depends on winning researchers and educators, that gap is worth addressing directly.

Not necessarily by changing the algorithm, but by being explicit about what drives retrieval and what that means for coverage.

What the Safety Model Actually Is

NotebookLM's help documentation warns that safety flags may block responses on sensitive topics, including violence and sexuality in historical contexts. Direct testing tells a different story.

Questions about Unit 731, the Rape of Nanking, wartime sexual violence, and post-war immunity decisions engaged without restriction. The actual safety model appears to be: answers must be traceable to sources, not topics must be safe. That's a more sophisticated and more defensible design, it makes the AI accountable to cited material rather than its own generation.

The problem is that Google's own documentation implies restriction where the product actually delivers. Researchers working with sensitive material who read the help text before testing the product may conclude it isn't suitable for their work, without ever discovering it is. For a product strategically targeting exactly those users, that's not a safety problem. It's a positioning problem.

What I'd Measure

Not sessions, not queries, not sources uploaded.

The metric that probably matters most is notebooks actively maintained across multiple sessions, a proxy for whether a user changed their research workflow, not just their chat behavior. A notebook that accumulates sources over time and gets queried repeatedly is evidence of real adoption. A notebook created once and abandoned is evidence the product impressed but didn't stick.

This is also where Deep Research's undisclosed selection criteria become a direct retention risk. If serious users encounter retrieved sources that don't hold up to scrutiny, multi-session engagement breaks down before the habit forms. The product's most important trust gap and its most important retention metric are directly connected, which means fixing one is an investment in the other.

The institutional integrations are the strongest structural response to this. A student required to build a notebook across a semester is set up for exactly the longitudinal, source-accumulating behavior that drives genuine retention. Google's path to sticky adoption may run through educators before it runs through individual users.

The Honest Summary

NotebookLM has a coherent product philosophy, a capability set that serves professional and academic research in ways general-purpose AI doesn't, and institutional traction that reflects a deliberate strategic bet on the users who matter most for enterprise adoption.

The competitive position is clear: where Perplexity is built for discovery, NotebookLM is built for depth. Users who understand that distinction get disproportionate value. Users who don't, churn.

Deep Research is the product's most important unresolved tension, not because the design is wrong, but because the selection logic is undisclosed and the users most exposed to that gap are the ones the product most needs to retain. The safety model is more capable than the documentation implies, which means Google is quietly underselling a genuine strength to exactly the users who would care most about it.

The product knows what it is. It hasn't fully explained that to the people it's built for.
