About

I started as someone who wanted to understand how systems work. I'm still that person, the systems just got more interesting.

The beginning, B.Tech CS

I came to engineering because I liked figuring out how things worked.

Not just the code, the logic underneath it. Why does this system behave this way? What breaks when you push it? Computer science gave me a language for asking those questions precisely. I didn't know then that I'd spend my career using that instinct on problems much messier than any codebase.

Microsoft, 4 years as a TPM

I got good at execution. Then I noticed what execution kept missing.

Four years shipping enterprise cloud products across global markets taught me what it means to coordinate complexity, 300+ stakeholders, five time zones, real organizations depending on things working. I got good at it.

But somewhere in there, I started noticing a gap nobody was owning: the space between what we were building and what users actually needed it to do. Engineers had their lane. Stakeholders had their asks. But the question of whether we were solving the right problem, that one floated. I kept stepping into it. Eventually I realized that wasn't a habit, it was a signal.

Then, in my final months at Microsoft, something shifted in the world. LLMs arrived at commercial scale, and every company I worked with started rushing to deploy AI in some form. What struck me wasn't the technology. It was the gap it exposed: companies knew they needed AI, but didn't know what problem they were actually solving for their customers. The technical ambition was everywhere. The product thinking was scarce. That gap was where I wanted to work.

UVA Darden, MBA

I went to business school to understand what drives the decisions I kept inheriting.

Four years of execution had taught me how businesses move. Darden taught me why they move that way, what drives innovation cycles, how companies differentiate, what makes a business decision actually rational given the constraints it's operating under.

But the most important thing I learned wasn't in a framework. It was a quieter realization: harm from technology is real, and it's usually a product decision. Not a policy failure, not an engineering bug, a product decision someone made, often with good intentions, without thinking through what happens to real users downstream. That changed what I think a PM's job actually is.

Now, what I'm looking for

I want to build products that solve real problems without introducing new ones.

That means AI-first companies where the technical decisions and the product decisions are genuinely intertwined, where responsibility has to be in the foundation, not bolted on at launch.

I'm open to both PM and TPM roles depending on the team and the problem. What I care about is being close enough to the product decisions to influence them, whether that's owning them outright or shaping the conditions that make them better.
